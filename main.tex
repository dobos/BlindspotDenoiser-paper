\begin{abstract}
Analyzing stellar spectra observed with at low signal-to-noise ratios (S/N) is unavoidable when survey targets are faint. Absorption-line measurements that determine stellar-parameter inference and target identification degrade first. Real surveys provide no paired clean references for machine learning, so supervised denoisers inherit a simulator-to-deployment dependency. We train a one-dimensional self-supervised blindspot denoiser on noisy spectra alone, predicting each wavelength bin from a receptive field that excludes that bin. On a survey-relevant simulated benchmark with heteroscedastic photon, sky, and read-out noise, the mean, per-spectrum reconstruction error (RMSE) falls by about a factor of $16$ from the noisy input to the blindspot output. To test whether this gain carries downstream information, we apply a surface gravity ($\log g$) estimator trained on clean synthetic spectra: the test set residual scatter falls from $1.26$~dex on noisy input to $0.80$~dex on the denoised output. As a comparison, validation-tuned classical smoothers stay at the noisy-input scale. This downstream test probes the information used for dwarf--giant  target classification, paramount in Galactic Archaeology, indicating that the self-supervised blindspot method recovers real information from the spectrum and denoised spectra are usable as input to traditional parameter estimation methods rather than only pixel-level reconstructions. The source code is publicly available on Github\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ViskaWei/BlindSpotDenoiser}}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
Galactic Archaeology analyses stellar spectra by measuring radial velocities, atmospheric parameters, and elemental abundances that trace the formation and evolution of stellar populations. Large multiplexed surveys of resolved stars typically pre-select targets from imaging catalogs before their membership can be confirmed spectroscopically \citep{Dey2019_LegacySurveys,Majewski2017_APOGEE}, so many faint members of the resulting samples are observed near the low signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) limit, where indiviual line measurements become fragile. Low signal-to-noise spectra directly limit target triage, follow-up strategy, and the scientific yield of the instrument. The effective signal-to-noise of the observations is further degraded by structured night-sky emission and wavelength-dependent atmospheric absorption \citep{Osterbrock1996_SkyAtlas,Hanuschik2003_SkyAtlas,Noll2012_ParanalSky}, both of which sit on top of the photon-noise floor that ultimately limits the recovery of the narrow absorption line features that carry the astrophysically useful information \citep{Bouchy2001_RVLimit}. A denoiser is useful at these S/N levels only if it reduces reconstruction error without erasing those line features; line preservation therefore frames the rest of this paper. Among the stellar labels, surface gravity is the most direct dwarf--giant discriminator and drives target membership decisions for faint Galactic Archaeology samples; surface gravity is therefore the downstream label tested in Section~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg}.

The main obstacle for deep-learning-based denoising in the low-S/N range is not neural network architecture, but the source of supervision. At the apparent magnitudes targeted here, real surveys do not provide noise-free spectra paired with every low-S/N exposure. Paired short exposures and deeper coadds can provide approximate noisy--less-noisy supervision when repeated observations exist, but such coadds are not clean references and are not available as a universal training target. A fully supervised denoiser therefore still typically depends on synthetic noisy--clean pairs and inherits a simulator-to-deployment dependency \citep{Pal2024_denoising_autoencoder}. Classical template-fitting methods and label models trained to obtain physical parameters match observed spectra against synthetic libraries and inherit the coverage limits of those libraries \citep{Koleva2009_ULySS,Ting2019_Payne}, while data-driven label models such as \textit{The Cannon} replace the synthetic library with labeled reference stars that span the target label space \citep{Ness2015_Cannon}. Each of these approaches relies, in a different way, on information that is not directly available from the noisy spectrum alone. A noisy-only training strategy, that requires neither clean targets nor a strong external prior, is the alternative this paper tests --- conditional on reducing reconstruction error without erasing the line structure that later analyses depend on. Whether blindspot prediction in the low-S/N stellar range tracks those line features, rather than smoothing them toward the local average, is the question this paper addresses.

Self-supervised denoising reduces this dependency by using the noise statistics of the observations instead of externally supplied clean spectra. If two independent, noisy observations of the same star have zero-mean errors, then averaging over the noisy targets recovers the same underlying spectrum in expectation \citep{lehtinen2018noise2noise}. Blindspot training replaces the second exposure by withholding the target wavelength bin and predicting its flux from the neighboring wavelength bins, so that the network cannot simply copy the noisy value \citep{batson2019noise2self,Krull2019_blindspot,Laine2019_BSN}. For spectra, this independence assumption must be stated in detector terms. The flux values are correlated among spectral pixels both because a stellar spectrum has continuum and line structure and because the instrumental line-spread function spreads light over neighboring bins, but the independence assumption holds about the noise, i.e. photon noise, read-out noise and thermal contributions are considered to be drawn independently for each detector pixel before removal of instrumental characteristics, sky subtraction, 1D spectrum extraction and fluxing. While data reduction steps usually introduce some correlation between neighboring pixels, we assume complete independence of the noise when generating the simulated training data.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We implementated a one-dimensional, self-supervised blindspot denoiser for low-S/N stellar spectra, trained from noisy spectra only and evaluated against clean references only after training.
    \item We generated simulated observations for training, validation and test data sets consisting of continuum-normalized BOSZ \citep{Bohlin2017} spectra at a resolution of $R\!\approx\!5000$ in the wavelength range of $7100$--$8850$~\AA and an apparent magnitude range of $20.5$--$22.5$. We used stellar parameters typical to late-type stars which are of the primary interest of Galactic Archaeology, and simulated a line spread function and noise characteristics typical to the Subaru Prime Focus Spectrograph (PFS) instrument.
    \item The main downstream result: We demonstated that a full-spectrum model trained on clean spectra to predict $\log g$ performs substantially better on blindspot-denoised spectra than on noisy or validation-tuned smoothed spectra, also improving dwarf--giant separation in faint Galactic Archaeology samples. Relative to the noisy input, the coefficient of determination rises from $0.01$ to $0.46$.
    \item We compared the performance of the blindspot denoiser against validation-tuned classical denoising filters, a denoiser based on principal-component analysis (PCA) using noisy input spectra and a non-deployable clean-template PCA reference, using aggregate reconstruction error (RMSE) as measure of performance.
    \item We performed line-preservation diagnostics by measuring the reconstruction error of the Ca\,\textsc{ii} triplet equivalent-width (EW) and an \mbox{O$_2$} A-band test in a high-noise atmospheric window.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
In spectroscopic terms, self-supervised denoising replaces the unavailable high-S/N reference spectrum with information from repeated noisy measurements and from the assumed noise statistics. \citet{lehtinen2018noise2noise} showed that pairs of independent noisy views of the same underlying signal suffice to recover the clean signal under zero-mean noise, removing the need for clean references at training time but still requiring a second observation. \citet{batson2019noise2self} reduced this requirement further by formulating denoising as a $\mathcal{J}$-invariant prediction task on a single noisy frame. Here $\mathcal{J}$-invariance means that the pixels are partitioned into held-out subsets and the prediction for any held-out pixel is computed only from the complementary pixels; the noisy target pixel is therefore unavailable to the predictor. \citet{Krull2019_blindspot} introduced a concrete single-image realization in which a randomly masked target pixel is predicted from its surrounding context so the noisy target cannot be copied through the receptive field of the predictor. \citet{Laine2019_BSN} converted this masking principle into a computationally efficient feed-forward architecture by combining branches whose receptive fields are restricted to disjoint half-spaces and then offset, so that the prediction at each output position never depends on the input at the same position. \citet{Krull2020_PN2V} extended the framework with an explicit pixel noise model and posterior inference rather than a single point estimate, while \citet{Xie2020_Noise2Same} revisited the underlying $\mathcal{J}$-invariance bound and replaced random masking with a self-consistency objective that does not require strict pixel exclusion. These methods establish the noisy-only training paradigm on two-dimensional natural images, but they leave two questions open for one-dimensional stellar spectra: whether the masked context retains enough information about the central wavelength to support useful prediction (a range question, addressed in Section~\ref{sec:theory} and Appendix~\ref{app:controls_gamma}), and whether the resulting estimator preserves the narrow absorption features that downstream stellar analysis depends on rather than smoothing them toward the local continuum (Section~\ref{sec:results_physical}).

A separate line of work has applied neural denoisers directly to astronomical spectra, in all cases relying on simulator-supplied clean references. \citet{Pal2024_denoising_autoencoder} train a one-dimensional denoising autoencoder on paired noisy and clean simulated stellar spectra and show that the recovered spectra speed up template-fit initialization, at the cost of requiring synthetic clean targets at training time. \citet{Scourfield2023_AEgalaxies} apply the same supervised paradigm to galaxy spectra with a noise-augmented variational autoencoder and report lower emission-line flux residuals against PCA baselines. \citet{Kang2021_bayesian_spectrum_denoise} take a different route: rather than mapping noisy to clean directly, they learn a prior over clean stellar spectra from a high-S/N reference set and combine it with a generative noise model in a Bayesian denoising procedure, which still requires the high-S/N reference set during training. The common limitation across these approaches is that the supervisory signal is available only inside the simulator or inside a high-S/N reference catalog, which is exactly the simulator-to-deployment dependency that motivates noisy-only training in this paper. Whether self-supervised blindspot training can match these reconstructions on stellar spectra, when judged jointly by aggregate reconstruction quality and by line-preservation diagnostics, has not previously been tested.

Classical spectroscopic parameter estimation methods usually address low-S/N observations through template matching or direct label inference rather than through denoising. Cross-correlation against a template library is the standard template-matching limit for radial-velocity estimation \citep{Tonry1979_xcorr,Kurtz1998_RVSAO}. Full-spectrum fitting is a more general version of the same idea: it fits many wavelength bins simultaneously, often allowing template mixtures, continuum terms, fluxing corrections, turbulent velocities, and dispersions, to recover stellar kinematics or population weights \citep{Koleva2009_ULySS,Cappellari2017_pPXFUpdate}. Data-driven label models, such as \textit{The Cannon}, and neural spectral models, such as \textit{The Payne}, learn the relation between stellar labels and spectral fluxes, so that labels for new spectra can be inferred using a learned spectral model rather than by matching each observation to a hand-built template grid \citep{Ness2015_Cannon,Ting2019_Payne}. These methods define the downstream use case for denoising: if a denoiser only improves visual smoothness or pixel-level error while destroying label-discriminating spectral structure, it is not useful for stellar analysis. We therefore treat template and label inference as downstream consumers of the reconstructed spectra, and we use a full-spectrum $\log g$ estimator trained on clean spectra and evaluated without refitting in Section~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg} as the main estimator-level test of whether the denoised spectra remain scientifically readable.

The contribution of this paper sits at the intersection of these three lines, not inside any one of them. From self-supervised denoising it inherits the blindspot construction and the requirement that the central pixel never enters into the prediction of itself. From neural spectrum denoising it inherits the goal of recovering line-sensitive structures at low S/N while removing the supervision dependency that limits deployment. Moreover, with respect to classical pipelines, it is positioned as upstream pre-processing rather than as a competing inference step.

Two questions follow that the rest of the paper addresses directly: whether one-dimensional medium-resolution stellar spectra satisfy the context-predictability premise in which the masked context is informationally close to the full context (Equation~\ref{eq:gamma}, Section~\ref{sec:theory}, and Appendix~\ref{app:context_recoverability}), and whether the resulting reconstruction preserves enough full-spectrum information to improve an unmodified downstream stellar-parameter estimator (Sections~\ref{sec:results_physical} and~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg}).

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Simulated Spectroscopic Observations}
The experiment uses simulated observations of medium resolution optical spectra derived from the BOSZ high-resolution synthetic stellar grid \citep{Bohlin2017,Meszaros2012}, based on ATLAS9 atmospheres \citep{Kurucz1979}. The synthetic spectrum is first convolved with the line-spread function and resampled to the detector pixels of the medium resolution spectrograph, as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}, then normalized by the same theoretical continuum curve $C(\lambda)$. The photon noise, dark noise and read-out noise variances were computed in the unit of detector counts before continuum normalization and, assuming Gaussian limit, a single noise variance is assigned per spectral pixel. We note, that for the faintest simulated targets the sky emission can dominate the noise budget. We assumed moonless night conditions only.

After normalization, both the flux and its uncertainty are divided by the same continuum:
\begin{equation}
    x_{\lambda}=\frac{F_{\lambda}}{C_{\lambda}},
    \qquad
    y_{\lambda}=\frac{F_{\lambda}+\epsilon_{F,\lambda}}{C_{\lambda}},
    \qquad
    \sigma_{\lambda}^{2}
    =
    \frac{\sigma_{F,\lambda}^{2}}{C_{\lambda}^{2}}.
    \label{eq:continuum_noise_scaling}
\end{equation}
Thus, the normalized noisy spectrum and the normalized uncertainty vector are on the same flux scale, and no continuum is fitted to the noisy or denoised spectra during evaluation. The magnitude and stellar-parameter ranges match those of the motivating Galactic Archeology survey: faint late-type spectra that remain informative despite being strongly noise dominated. Simulation parameters are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}.

\subsection{Noise Model}
The observation simulator outputs the wavelength grid, continuum-normalized clean flux $x$, continuum-normalized noisy flux $y$, and the per-pixel uncertainty vector. The per-pixel variance is calculated as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{\lambda}^{2}
    =
    \sigma_{\mathrm{obj},\lambda}^{2}
    +
    \sigma_{\mathrm{sky},\lambda}^{2}
    +
    \sigma_{\mathrm{read},\lambda}^{2},
    \label{eq:noise_components}
\end{equation}
where each term is normalized with the same continuum.
The three terms encode noise from object photons, photon noise from the structured sky background, and detector read and dark noise as transfered by the telescope and the detector. \ld{maybe elaborate on the transfer function a bit more}

We note, that in our simulations, $\m{\sigma}_{\mathrm{obj}}$ is a deterministic function of the clean flux $\m{x}$ which can have implications for the training of the blindspot denoiser when $\m{\sigma}$ is used as input.

\subsection{Problem Formulation and Blindspot Objective}
\label{sec:problem_formulation}

Let $\m{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{N}$ be a clean spectrum and $\m{y}\in\mathbb{R}^{N}$ its noisy observation. In components, the noisy spectrum is
\begin{align}
    y_{\lambda} &= x_{\lambda} + \epsilon_{\lambda} \\[1em]
    \epsilon_{\lambda} &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\lambda}^{2}),
    \label{eq:noise_model_scalar}
\end{align}
where $\sigma_\lambda^2$ is the variance of the noise at wavelength bin $\lambda$ and $\lambda = 1,2,\dots, N$ indexes the wavelength bins.

The task is to learn the $\m{\theta}$ parameters of a denoiser $f_{\m{\theta}}$ from noisy spectra only: during training, $\m{y}$ is used but $\m{x}$ is not. A blindspot predictor estimates each wavelength bin from its surrounding context with the center bin excluded:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x}_{\lambda} 
        = f_{\m{\theta}}(\m{y}_{-\lambda},\m{\sigma}_{-\lambda})
\end{equation}
Here $\m{y}_{-\lambda}$ and $\m{\sigma}_{-\lambda}$ denote the receptive-field input after excluding $y_{\lambda}$ and $\sigma_{\lambda}$, whereas $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$ is the point estimate returned by the network, and $\m{\theta}$ denotes all trainable weights and biases of the denoiser.

As we noted before, $\sigma_{\lambda}$ is a deterministic function of the clean flux $x_\lambda$ in our simulations, so feeding the full vector $\m{\sigma}$ into the network would leak information about the clean flux into the prediction. To avoid this, we summarize the per-bin variances into a single scalar $\bar{\sigma}$ and use it as input to the network:
\begin{align}
    \hat{x}_{\lambda} 
        &= f_{\m{\theta}}(\m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma})
        \label{eq:blindspot_prediction} \\
    \bar{\sigma}^2
        &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{\lambda=1}^{N}\sigma_{\lambda}^{2}.
\end{align}

The scalar $\bar{\sigma}$ represents the overall noise level of the spectrum and $\bar{\sigma}$ has the same unit of flux as $y_{\lambda}$. We use the sum of the per-bin variances rather than the full vector $\m{\sigma}$ because the full vector contains wavelength-specific information from the noiseless simulated spectrum that could cause data leakage. Hence the full, pixel-wise variance vector is used only to draw $\epsilon_{\lambda}$ in Equation~\ref{eq:noise_model_scalar} and the network receives only the scalar noise summary $\bar{\sigma}$, as defined in Equation~\ref{eq:blindspot_prediction}. The clean reference $\m{x}$ is completely withheld during training and used only for evaluation. \ld{review: In a real-survey deployment, the same input channel can carry the survey pipeline's noise estimate without changing the network architecture. A Bayesian fusion of the network prediction with the noisy observation is possible in principle, but because the variance of the per-pixel noise level is negligible in the present setting, we do not apply it and take the network mean $\hat{x}$ directly as the denoised output.}


We train the network with a noisy-target Huber loss in the form of
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}(\m{\theta};\m{y})
    &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{\lambda=1}^{N}
        \ell_{\delta}\!\left(y_{\lambda} - \hat{x}_{\lambda}\right) \\[1em]
    \ell_{\delta}(r) &=
    \begin{cases}
        \tfrac{1}{2}r^{2} & |r| \leq \delta,\\
        \delta\!\left(|r| - \tfrac{1}{2}\delta\right) & |r| > \delta.
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:self_supervised_loss}
\end{align}
The Huber loss behaves as squared-error regression for residuals with $|r|\leq\delta$, but grows only linearly for larger residuals. It therefore keeps the usual mean regression behavior near the optimum while reducing the influence of outliers and rare pixels with large noise.

The optimization problem can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \m{\theta}^\ast = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\m{\theta}}\;\mathbb{E}_{y}\!\left[\mathcal{L}(\m{\theta};\m{y})\right],
    \label{eq:theta_star}
\end{equation}
where the expectation value is taken over the ensemble of noisy spectra. The denoised spectrum is obtained by evaluating Equation~\ref{eq:blindspot_prediction} at $\m{\theta}^\ast$.

Note, that neither clean fluxes, nor per-pixel inverse-variance weights enter the training loss. The pixelwise variance vector is compressed into the scalar $\bar{\sigma}$ before it is supplied to the network and it is not used at all in the loss function.

Under the symmetric zero-mean per-pixel noise model of Equation~\ref{eq:noise_model_scalar}, and for a Huber breakpoint $\delta$ chosen to be large relative to the typical residuals, the population minimizer is approximately
\begin{equation}
    f^\ast(\m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma})
    \approx
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[y_{\lambda}\mid \m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma}\right]
    =
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[x_{\lambda}\mid \m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma}\right].
    \label{eq:population_target}
\end{equation}
The equality follows because the noise has zero conditional mean. Asymmetric or heavy-tailed noise, or a $\delta$ comparable to or smaller than the typical magnitude of the residuals, would shift the optimum toward an outlier-attenuated conditional location rather than the conditional mean.

\subsection{Blindspot Network Architecture}
\label{sec:blindspot_algorithm}

A blindspot neural network can follow any feed-forward architecture 
as long as the forward pass ensures one invariant: the feature used to predict the flux in bin $\lambda$ is never computed from $y_{\lambda}$. When feature extraction from the input is done with a convolutional network, this property is often referred to as \textit{causality}. To compute some internal features that will be used downstream to predict $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$, the one-dimensional blindspot network uses two identical, but mirrored causal branches. The left branch calculates the feature vector $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{L} = U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{L})$, where $\m{z}^{L} = \m{y}_{<\lambda}$ is the input spectrum in the normal order of wavelength bins for all $\lambda_i < \lambda$, so the extracted features at $\lambda$ depend only on $\m{y}_{<\lambda}$. The right branch processes the wavelength-reversed spectrum for all $\lambda_i > \lambda$ and calculates $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{R} = U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{R})$, so the right-side features at $\lambda$ depend only on $\m{y}_{>\lambda}$. The prediction head, that calculates $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$, receives only the $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{L}$ and $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{R}$ feature vectors, therefore, no computational path carries $y_{\lambda}$ into $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$.

\begin{figure}
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics{figs/network.pdf}
    \end{center}
    \caption{Illustration of the blindspot network architecture for the prediction of a single spectrum pixel $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$. The two UNet blocks of $U_{\theta}$ are identical with the same weights but their receptive fields are only $\m{y}_{<\lambda}$ and $\m{y}_{>\lambda}$, respectively and $y_{\lambda}$ is excluded from both. The left block processes the input spectrum in increasing wavelength order, while the input of the right block is reversed in wavelength order. The two feature vectors $\m{u}^{L}$ and $\m{u}^{R}$ are concatenated and passed to the pointwise head $g_{\theta}$ to predict $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$. For simplicity, the figure shows only the flux channel, but the network can also accept a second channel for the variance. We input the mean of the variance vector $\bar{\sigma}$ to both branches, so that the network can adapt to the overall noise level of the spectrum but it does not get the individual pixel variances.}
    \label{fig:network}
\end{figure}

The input to the blindspot network is a 1-dimensional array $\m{y}\in\mathbb{R}^{N}$ or the stacked array $\{y_\lambda, \sigma_\lambda\}_{\lambda=1}^{N} \in\mathbb{R}^{2 \times N}$ representing the continuum-normalized flux and the pixel-wise variance, ordered by wavelength. To select a specific $\lambda$ for which the prediction is made, the input is padded so that the receptive field of the network covers only $\m{y}_{<\lambda}$ and $\m{y}_{>\lambda}$, but not $y_{\lambda}$. The output of the network is $\hat{x}_\lambda$. The architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:network}. When two separate channels for the flux and the variance are used, two feature vectors are created by $U_{\m{\theta}}$, and both are fed into the pointwise head $g_{\m{\theta}}$ to predict $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$.

In case of simulated spectra, to avoid data leakage, the pixel-wise variance vector $\{\sigma_\lambda\}_{\lambda=1}^{N}$ is substituted with the constant vector $\bar{\sigma}$, which is the mean of the pixel-wise variance vector.

The blindspot network is evaluated in the following steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Route the noisy spectrum $\m{y}$ and $\bar{\m{\sigma}}$ in the two wavelength directions:
        \begin{equation*}
            \m{z}^{L}\leftarrow \{\m{y}, \bar{\m{\sigma}}\}
            \quad \text{and} \quad
            \m{z}^{R}\leftarrow \{\mathrm{rev}(\m{y}), \bar{\m{\sigma}}\}
        \end{equation*}
    \item Apply the same $U_{\m{\theta}}$ to each branch shifted to the wavelength of interest:
        \begin{equation*}
            \m{u}^{L}\leftarrow U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{L}),
            \quad
            \tilde{\m{u}}^{R}\leftarrow U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{R})
        \end{equation*}
    \item Restore the right-branch features to the original wavelength order:
        \begin{equation*}
            \m{u}^{R}\leftarrow\mathrm{rev}(\tilde{\m{u}}^{R}),
        \end{equation*}

\subsection{Identifiability and Recoverability}
\label{sec:theory}

Throughout this section we work under the heteroscedastic Poisson--Gaussian noise model of Section~\ref{sec:implementation}, where the noise has zero conditional mean even though its variance depends on wavelength and flux.

\paragraph{Blindspot exclusion prevents the identity shortcut}
If the center pixel was visible, the noisy-target objective would have a zero-loss copy solution:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x}_{\lambda}=y_{\lambda}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    \mathcal{L}(\m{\theta};\m{y})
    = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\lambda=1}^{L}
        \ell_{\delta}\!\left(y_{\lambda}-y_{\lambda}\right)
    =0.
\end{equation}
This solution contains no information about $x_{\lambda}$. The blindspot construction eliminates this problem by explicitly excluding $y_{\lambda}$ from the receptive field of the network.

\paragraph{Identifiability follows from zero-mean noise} Let $\Omega_{\lambda} = (\m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma})$ denote the blindspot context. Identifiability follows from
\begin{align}
    y_{\lambda} = x_{\lambda} + \epsilon_{\lambda} \\[1em]
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[\epsilon_{\lambda} \mid x_{\lambda},\Omega_{\lambda}\right] = 0,
    \label{eq:consistency_setup}
\end{align}
which gives
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[y_{\lambda}\mid \Omega_{\lambda}\right]
    \;=\;
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[x_{\lambda}\mid \Omega_{\lambda}\right].
    \label{eq:consistency}
\end{equation}
The unpredictable noise washes out under the conditional expectation, leaving the context-conditioned clean signal as the optimal regression target.

\paragraph{Loss-function caveat}
Equation~\ref{eq:consistency} characterizes the conditional mean as the optimal MSE regression target. The trained model uses the Huber loss $\ell_{\delta}$ in Equation~\ref{eq:self_supervised_loss} for outlier robustness. As $\delta\to\infty$, this loss reduces to MSE and the population optimum coincides with the conditional mean, while at finite $\delta$, the optimum is a Huber M-estimator that approximates the conditional mean when residuals are sub-Gaussian relative to the breakpoint. We therefore use Equation~\ref{eq:consistency} as the leading-order target in the magnitude range of Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}, not as an unconditional exact minimizer outside that range.

\paragraph{Recoverability is measured by a context-residual ratio}
The blindspot construction is useful only when the spectral pixels surrounding the blind spot determine the clean flux at the hidden wavelength better than the noisy observation itself. We measure the recoverability by the ratio between two variances: let $\tau_\lambda^{2}$ denote the conditional variance of the clean flux after observing the blindspot context, whereas we keep using $\sigma_\lambda^{2}$ to denote the variance of the noise in the withheld pixel:
\begin{align}
    \tau_\lambda^{2} &= \mathrm{Var}(x_{\lambda}\mid\Omega_{\lambda}) \\[1em]
    \sigma_\lambda^{2} &= \mathrm{Var}(\epsilon_{\lambda}).
\end{align}
With these, we define the ratio 
\begin{align}
    \gamma_\lambda &=\; \frac{\tau_\lambda^{2}}{\sigma_\lambda^{2}}.
    \label{eq:gamma}
\end{align}
A small $\gamma_\lambda$ means the context predicts the clean flux more accurately than the noisy center observation itself, so the real flux at the hidden wavelength is recoverable in the sense needed for blindspot denoising. \ld{???: In this heteroscedastic simulator, $\gamma$ is a spectrum-level recoverability summary whose per-pixel interpretation is recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:gamma_heteroscedasticity}.}

The target support has a physically set correlation scale. The reason to expect small $\gamma_\lambda$ is not generic smoothness, but the ratio between the spectral correlation length and the variance of the noise. \ld{Why?} The spectral correlation length is determined jointly by stellar physics via intrinsic line width and by the instrument via the line-spread function.

\subsection{Information limits on downstream tasks}

In order to estimate a stellar parameter $\varphi$ from a noisy spectrum, the Fisher information matrix $\mathcal{I}_{\varphi}$ quantifies the amount of information that the noisy observation $\m{y}$ carries about the parameter $\phi$. For scalar $\varphi$, the Fisher information takes the form of
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}_{\varphi}(\m{y}) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \varphi}\log p(\m{y}\mid\varphi)\right)^{2}\right],
\end{equation}
where $p(\m{y}\mid\varphi)$ is the likelihood of the noisy observation $\m{y}$ given the parameter $\varphi$. The Fisher information matrix is a lower bound on the covariance of any unbiased estimator of $\varphi$ through the Cramér-Rao bound. The denoiser network $f_\theta$ is a deterministic transformation of the noisy observation $\m{y}$ into a denoised spectrum $\hat{\m{x}}$, hence the information about $\varphi$ in the denoised spectrum $\hat{\m{x}}$ cannot exceed the information in the noisy observation $\m{y}$, which is expressed by the data processing inequality
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}_{\varphi}(\m{y}) \geq \mathcal{I}_{\varphi}(\hat{\m{x}}),
\end{equation}
and the inequality is strict unless $f_\theta$ is invertible, but this is not the case for a blindspot denoiser. The Fisher information matrix therefore provides a theoretical upper bound on the performance of any downstream estimator that uses the denoised spectrum $\hat{\m{x}}$ to estimate the parameter $\varphi$.

\subsection{Training}
The denoiser network we trained consisted of a single U-Net backbone $U_{\m{\theta}}$ with four input channels for feature extraction and and a prediction head of \ld{densely connected layers}. The backbone had 9 convolutional layers with a kernel size of $25$ and a total number of 48 kernels. The four input channels were the left and right shifted causal branches with proper padding and the two channels of constant vectors filled with the scalar noise parameter $\bar{\sigma}$. The U-Net backbone had an output of 2 times 48 channels, the $\m{u}^{L}$ and $\m{u}^{R}$ feature vectors, which were concatenated into a single channel by the prediction head. In the U-Net backbone, we used batch normalization and residual aggregation between matching encoder and decoder levels. The prediction head $g_{\m{\theta}}$ consisted of 3 convolutional layers with kernel size of $1$ to mix down the U-Net output to a single channel that predicts the denoised flux $\hat{x}$. The U-Net backbone used ReLU activation functions.

The networks was trained with the Huber loss of Eq.~\ref{eq:self_supervised_loss} with \ld{$\delta=0.1$}. Optimization was done using an Adam optimizer with $(\beta_1,\beta_2,\epsilon)=(0.9,0.999,10^{-8})$ and zero weight decay, initial learning rate $10^{-4}$, and a cosine schedule decaying to $10^{-6}$ over the full training horizon. Training used a maximum of $200$ epochs with a batch size of $128$ and a single fixed training seed. The early-stopping patience was set to $30$ epochs and the model with the smallest Huber loss on the validation set was chosen as the best-fit model, before any test set evaluation. At each training step, $85\%$ of wavelength bins were sampled at random and contributed to the loss function, while the remaining bins were held out. This stochastic masking is the standard for training blindspot networks in order to decorrelate the predictions for successive wavelength bins. During test set evaluation, the full spectra were used to calculate the Huber loss. We note, that while the Huber loss was used for training the network, we report the RMSE of the denoised spectra in the results section, which is a more standard metric for the reconstruction error.

The detailed training procedure was as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item For each epoch and each mini-batch of noisy spectra, calculate the scalar noise parameter
        \begin{equation*}
            \left( \bar{\sigma}^{(j)} \right)^2 = L^{-1}\sum_{\lambda} \left( \sigma_{\lambda}^{(j)} \right)^{2}.
        \end{equation*}
        A constant vector $\bar{\m{\sigma}}^{(j)}$ formed from this value is used as the second input channel to the network.
    \item Calculate the prediction for each wavelength bin with a blindspot forward pass:
        \begin{equation*}
            \hat{x}_{\lambda}^{(j)} = f_{\theta}(\m{y}_{-\lambda}^{(j)},\bar{\m{\sigma}}^{(j)}).
        \end{equation*}
    \item Generate a random mask of $85\%$ of the wavelength bins and calculate the Huber loss of Equation~\ref{eq:self_supervised_loss} only for the masked bins.
    \item For each mini-batch, calculate the gradient and update the network parameters $\theta$ with the Adam optimizer.
    \item After each epoch, evaluate the Huber loss on the validation set and save the model if it is the best so far.
    \item Repeat until early stopping criteria are met or a maximum number of epochs is reached.
\end{enumerate}

We trained the blindspot denoiser in two configurations: one with a single-channel input for the noisy flux only, and one with a two-channel input for the noisy flux and the constant $\bar{\sigma}$ vector which is just a summary of the per-pixel variance vector.

While PCA denoising is a simple, well-understood linear baseline, it is based entirely on clean reference spectra and the limited number of principal components are not sufficient to capture the full complexity of stellar absorption features. \ld{
Because not all distributions we encounter are Gaussian, we use the robust scatter $\sigma_{\rm rob}$ as a measure of the spread of the residuals between the predicted and true $\log g$ values. The robust scatter is defined as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{\rm rob} = 1.4826 \cdot \mathrm{median}(|\Delta - \mathrm{median}(\Delta)|),
\end{equation}
where $\Delta = \hat{x} - x$ is the residual between the predicted value $\hat{x}$ and the true value $x$. The factor $1.4826$ scales the median absolute deviation to be comparable to the standard deviation for a Gaussian distribution. When use to measure the error of a predicted parameter, a smaller $\sigma_{\rm rob}$ indicates better performance of the parameter estimator.
}


The coefficient of determination $R^2$ measures the proportion of variance in the true $\log g$ values that is explained by the predicted values. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
    R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \hat{x}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \bar{x})^2},
\end{equation}
where $x_i$ is the true value, $\hat{x}_i$ is the predicted value, $\bar{x}$ is the mean of the true values, and $N$ is the number of data points. An $R^2$ value of $1$ indicates perfect predictions, while a value of $0$ indicates that the model does no better than predicting the mean of the true values.

The value of $R^{2}$ for the clean reference in Table~\ref{tab:downstream_logg} establishes that the LightGBM model is capable of extracting gravity information from clean spectra sufficiently well, whereas the coefficient is almost zero for the noisy input and very close to zero for the smoothed input. The blindspot-denoised spectra achieve an $R^{2}$ of $0.46$, which is a significant improvement over the noisy input and the smoothed input, but still substantially below the clean-reference performance. This indicates that our $\log g$ estimator is not robust against noise, yet the blindspot denoiser is able to recover enough information from the noisy spectra to improve $\log g$ estimation.

\label{sec:discussion}

In this simulated target support, noisy-only blindspot training lowers the mean reconstruction RMSE from $0.084$ on the noisy input to $0.0053$ on test spectra, below validation-tuned classical filters, noisy-PCA, and a non-deployable clean-template PCA reference. The line-sensitive diagnostics in Section~\ref{sec:results_physical} show that this gain is not obtained by simply filling absorption features toward the continuum: Ca\,\textsc{ii} EW errors decrease substantially. The full-spectrum calibrator in Section~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg} then provides the end-to-end result: after training only on clean spectra, the $\log g$ estimator reads substantially more label information from blindspot-denoised spectra than from noisy or conventionally smoothed spectra.

\paragraph{Rest-frame alignment as a deployment cost.} The reconstruction ceiling of the linear baselines in Table~\ref{tab:main_baselines} --- a property of the estimator class rather than of tuning, as Appendix~\ref{app:controls_gamma} shows --- is sometimes read as a mere preprocessing gap: shift every spectrum to its rest frame first, and the basis would no longer have to span a separate velocity copy of each line. That repair is not available in the setting this paper targets. Rest-frame resampling needs the per-spectrum radial velocity, and recovering it without a clean template reintroduces the supervised dependency that the noisy-only construction is built to remove. At the faint magnitudes that motivate denoising, a velocity estimated from the noisy spectrum itself is uncertain, and that uncertainty propagates into every realigned pixel. Granting an exact velocity does not make the alignment free either. Resampling onto a common grid is an interpolation that forms each output pixel from a weighted sum of neighbors. This couples the previously independent per-pixel noise draws that the consistency argument of Section~\ref{sec:theory} depends on, and it low-pass filters the narrow line cores, broadening and shallowing exactly the high-frequency, low-variance structure the fixed basis already cannot recover. Alignment thus either reintroduces clean-reference supervision or degrades the signal it is meant to expose. The blindspot estimator never faces this choice: it predicts each bin from the local shape of its neighborhood with the center withheld, so a global velocity shift slides the evaluation window without altering the learned line-shape-to-flux map. Its invariance to radial velocity is built into the estimator rather than supplied by a preprocessing stage that must first be made to work.

\paragraph{Scope of the simulated benchmark.} The evaluated setting is the independent zero-mean simulator of Section~\ref{sec:method}, with continuum-normalized late-type spectra and the PFS-like wavelength, resolution, and magnitude range of Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}. Real survey deployment introduces correlated sky-subtraction residuals, extraction covariance, and a varying instrumental line-spread function that are not part of the noise model tested here. Continuum-estimation error is also out of scope: the analysis is conditioned on continuum-normalized spectra produced by the simulator, so the systematic continuum-fit error that real surveys must absorb does not enter any of the metrics. Carrying the same construction to survey-specific residuals or to other stellar supports requires reusing the same evaluation protocol: select the denoiser without clean targets, hold it fixed, and then test reconstruction, line preservation, and downstream clean-trained transfer on a disjoint evaluation set.

\paragraph{Reproducibility scope.} All main numerical results are obtained from one selected checkpoint evaluated on the z1/test\_10k\_1 split, with a single noise realization per spectrum. The repeated-noise diagnostic in Appendix~\ref{app:noise_stability} tests sensitivity to noise draws for a fixed clean spectrum; it is not uncertainty calibration and it does not substitute for a full training-seed robustness study. Independent training-seed repeats, scalar-noise-conditioning ablations, and supervised clean-target references are left outside the main claim unless evaluated under the same pre-specified metric battery.

\paragraph{Copy-shortcut control.} The copy shortcut is ruled out structurally by the blindspot construction and numerically by the integrity audit detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:architecture_integrity}; we do not run a separate central-visible/noisy-target reconstruction control.

\paragraph{Downstream information preservation.} The clean-trained $\log g$ calibrator reads from the full flux vector rather than from the six equivalent-width diagnostics, and is never retrained on noisy or denoised spectra. Its lower residuals on blindspot-denoised input therefore record a transfer property of the denoised representation, not a tuning artefact. The validation-tuned smoothers, scored against the same fixed calibrator, do not produce the same improvement, which separates the line-preserving content of the blindspot reconstruction from generic smoothness. A reduction in $\log g$ residual scatter is not the same object as a thresholded dwarf--giant confusion matrix; that classification metric is not reported here.

\paragraph{Estimator-dependent score.} The downstream gain reported here is measured under a full-spectrum gradient-boosted regressor trained on clean spectra and evaluated without refitting. Line-window-restricted tangent-risk diagnostics in companion benchmark work can report different scores under the same simulator family because they index the denoiser error by a declared local-linear evaluation map rather than by a full-spectrum nonlinear parameter calibrator. The two readings should not be averaged or substituted for one another: the present result supports usefulness for this $\log g$ calibrator, while line-window diagnostics answer a different local-fidelity question.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This study shows that a one-dimensional blindspot denoiser can turn noisy late-type spectra into reconstructions that preserve absorption-line structure and improve clean-trained gravity transfer on a controlled BOSZ/PFS-like benchmark. Clean spectra do not enter the denoiser training loss, and the target wavelength is excluded from the receptive field. Under the selected checkpoint and fixed evaluation protocol, the evidence separates into three claims: reconstruction improves, line diagnostics remain favorable, and a surface-gravity estimator trained on clean spectra transfers better without refitting.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reconstruction gain.} On the $10{,}000$-spectrum reconstruction test split, mean per-spectrum reconstruction RMSE falls from $0.084$ for the noisy input to $0.0053$ for the blindspot output, a factor of about $16$. The central-visible control remains at the noisy-input scale ($0.084$), supporting the interpretation that target-bin exclusion prevents a copy shortcut rather than merely adding network capacity.
    \item \textbf{Line preservation.} Median Ca\,\textsc{ii} relative EW errors for the $8498$, $8542$, and $8662$~\AA{} lines fall to $0.121$, $0.098$, and $0.147$. These diagnostics argue that the reconstruction retains local absorption-line contrast rather than only improving the global norm.
    \item \textbf{Estimator transfer.} For a full-spectrum surface-gravity estimator trained on clean spectra and evaluated without refitting on a disjoint $5{,}000$-spectrum test partition, robust scatter falls from $1.26$ to $0.80$~dex and $R^{2}$ rises from $0.01$ to $0.46$. This is an estimator-specific transfer gain, not full stellar-label recovery.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these results make the case for blindspot denoising as a candidate preprocessing step for faint late-type spectra, with usefulness measured by fixed downstream transfer rather than by visual smoothness alone. The natural next validation is to repeat the same fixed-estimator transfer test on observed spectra, or on simulations that include structured sky residuals, extraction covariance, line-spread-function variation, and continuum-estimation error.

\paragraph{Consolidated per-method results.}
Reconstruction RMSE decreases with estimator capacity, but the downstream $\log g$ estimator separates methods that preserve label information from methods that only reduce pixel error: the principal-component references reach low reconstruction RMSE among the non-neural methods yet reduce the fixed calibrator's downstream accuracy relative to the noisy-input row, whereas the blindspot moves the calibrator most of the way to the clean-input scale.

\paragraph{Why the fixed-basis linear estimators saturate.} The reconstruction ceiling of the principal-component baselines in Table~\ref{tab:main_baselines} is a property of the estimator class, not of how the basis was tuned. A fixed-grid linear basis must spend rank on two demands at once. Each spectrum carries a radial velocity of up to $\pm300$~km/s (Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}), which slides its absorption lines by several resolution elements, and representing an arbitrary shift on a fixed wavelength grid requires many Fourier modes rather than a few stellar-parameter directions. The narrow line cores are themselves high-frequency, low-variance features that again demand high rank. Both pressures push toward a large basis. Yet at the faint-end per-pixel S/N of this sample, every added component admits more noise variance than line variance, so the reconstruction-error-optimal rank is small: validation selects $k=16$ across the search range $\{16,\dots,512\}$. The linear class is therefore caught in a bias--variance bind, where the rank the signal structure demands is the rank the noise forbids, and no choice of rank --- with or without access to clean training targets --- escapes it. This is why the clean-template reference, fit on noiseless spectra, still floors at a reconstruction RMSE of $0.011$. The blindspot estimator is not a fixed-basis projection: it predicts each bin from the local shape of its neighborhood with the center withheld, so its accuracy is set by the physical correlation length of the signal relative to the per-bin noise (Section~\ref{sec:theory}) rather than by a subspace dimension, and a global velocity shift leaves the learned line-shape-to-flux map unchanged.

\paragraph{Heteroscedasticity and scalar $\gamma$.}
\label{app:gamma_heteroscedasticity}
The simulator draws $\epsilon_{\lambda}$ with the per-bin variance $\sigma_{\lambda}^{2}$ of Equation~\ref{eq:noise_components}, while the denoiser receives only the spectrum-level RMS noise condition $\bar{\sigma}$ of Equation~\ref{eq:blindspot_prediction}. The reported $\gamma$ values are therefore pixelwise residual-variance ratios summarized over wavelength, not a homoscedastic noise assumption. Windows whose variance profile differs from the spectrum-level summary are checked explicitly through the Ca\,\textsc{ii}, \mbox{O$_2$}, and TiO columns of Table~\ref{tab:gamma_full} and through the line-preservation diagnostics in Section~\ref{sec:results_physical}.

\paragraph{Risk interpretation of $\gamma$.}
\label{app:gamma_risk_interpretation}
The main text uses $\gamma$ only as a recoverability condition. In the local Gaussian model, the same ratio also gives the explicit comparison among three estimators of $x_{\lambda}$:
\[
    R_{\rm raw} = \sigma^{2},
    \qquad
    R_{\rm BS} = \tau^{2} = \gamma\,\sigma^{2},
    \qquad
    R_{\rm full} = \frac{\gamma}{1+\gamma}\,\sigma^{2}.
\]
The corresponding ratios are
\[
    \frac{R_{\rm raw}}{R_{\rm BS}} = \frac{1}{\gamma},
    \qquad
    \frac{R_{\rm BS}}{R_{\rm full}} = 1+\gamma,
    \qquad
    \frac{R_{\rm raw}}{R_{\rm full}} = 1 + \frac{1}{\gamma}.
\]
Thus, when $\gamma\ll1$, the context-only blindspot conditional-mean estimator is close to the center-visible conditional-mean estimator, while both are far better than leaving the noisy pixel unchanged. This calculation is only an interpretation of the measured context-residual ratio; it is not used as a substitute for the test-set reconstruction and line-preservation tests in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
