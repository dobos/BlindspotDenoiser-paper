\begin{abstract}
Analyzing stellar spectra observed at low signal-to-noise ratios ($\SNR$) is unavoidable when survey targets are faint. Absorption line measurements that are essential to determine the stellar parameters and identify stellar types degrade first. We investigate weather sophisticated neural-network-based denoisers can help mitigate this. Real surveys provide no paired clean references for machine learning, so supervised denoisers inherit a simulator-to-deployment dependency. Although we still use simulated spectra, we train a one-dimensional, convolutional, self-supervised blindspot denoiser on noisy spectra alone, without using the clean reference during training or validation. Our blindspot network can predict the flux in every wavelength bin from a receptive field that excludes that particular wavelength bin from the input. With heteroscedastic noise, the mean, per-spectrum reconstruction error (RMSE) falls by about a factor of \ld{$16$} from the noisy input to the blindspot output. The uncertainty of equivalent widths of absorption features decrease by a factor of \ld{$XX$}. To test whether this gain carries downstream information relevant for Galactic Archeology surveys, we apply classical machine learning models trained on clean synthetic spectra to predict the athmospheric parameters.

This downstream test probes the information used for dwarf--giant  target classification, paramount in Galactic Archaeology, indicating that the self-supervised blindspot method recovers real information from the spectrum and denoised spectra are usable as input to traditional parameter estimation methods rather than only pixel-level reconstructions.}
The source code of the blindspot denoiser is publicly available on Github\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ViskaWei/BlindSpotDenoiser}}.

Galactic Archaeology analyses stellar spectra by measuring radial velocities, atmospheric parameters, and elemental abundances that trace the formation and evolution of stellar populations. Large multiplexed surveys of resolved stars typically pre-select targets from imaging catalogs before their membership can be confirmed spectroscopically \citep{Dey2019_LegacySurveys,Majewski2017_APOGEE}, so many faint members of the resulting samples are observed near the low $\SNR$ limit, where indiviual line measurements become fragile. Low signal-to-noise spectra directly limit target triage, follow-up strategy, and the scientific yield of the instrument. At magnitudes fainter than $g > 22$, the $\SNR$ of the observations is primarily determined by the continuum sky emission in the blue and the continuum and strong emission lines in the red and near infrared bands. Telluric absorption \citep{Osterbrock1996_SkyAtlas,Hanuschik2003_SkyAtlas,Noll2012_ParanalSky} is severe in some relatively broad wavelength ranges which further increases the photon noise floor and ultimately limits the recovery of the narrow absorption line features that carry the astrophysically useful information \citep{Bouchy2001_RVLimit}. A denoiser is useful at these $\SNR$ levels only if it reduces reconstruction error without erasing the line features; verifying line preservation therefore frames the rest of this paper. Among the stellar labels, surface gravity is the most direct dwarf--giant discriminator and drives target membership decisions for faint Galactic Archaeology samples, \ld{surface gravity is therefore the primary label tested in Section~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg}}.

The main obstacle for deep-learning-based denoising in the low-$\SNR$ regime is not neural network architecture, but the source of supervision. At the apparent magnitude ranges investigated here, typically $23 \geq g \geq 21.5$, real surveys cannot, even by coadding multiple exposures, achieve a high enough $\SNR$ so that noisy spectra can be paired with high quality counterparts to be used as a training set. A supervised denoiser, therefore, typically depends on synthetic noisy--clean pairs and inherits a simulator-to-deployment dependency \citep{Pal2024_denoising_autoencoder}. Classical template-fitting methods and label models trained to obtain physical parameters match observed spectra against synthetic libraries and inherit the coverage limits of those libraries \citep{Koleva2009_ULySS,Ting2019_Payne}, while data-driven label models such as \textit{The Cannon} replace the synthetic library with labeled reference stars that span the target label space \citep{Ness2015_Cannon}. Each of these approaches relies, in a different way, on information that is not directly available from the noisy spectrum alone. A noisy-only training strategy of a denoiser, that requires neither clean targets, nor a strong external prior, is the alternative this paper tests.

Self-supervised denoising removes the dependency on externally supplied clean reference spectra by learning the noise statistics from the observations. If independent, noisy observations of the same star have zero-mean errors in each wavelength bin, then averaging over many noisy spectra bin-by-bin should recover the same underlying spectrum in expectation \citep{lehtinen2018noise2noise}. Blindspot training replaces the repeated exposures by withholding the target wavelength bin from the input and predicting its flux from the neighboring wavelength bins, so that the network cannot simply copy the noisy value \citep{batson2019noise2self,Krull2019_blindspot,Laine2019_BSN} from the input.

Convolutional denoisers depend on correlations of the signal in neighboring wavelength bins which correlations originate from physics and instrumental effects. The flux values are correlated among spectral pixels both because a stellar spectrum has continuum and line structure and because the instrumental line-spread function distributes light over neighboring bins, but the independence assumption holds about the noise, i.e. photon noise, read-out noise and thermal contributions are considered to be drawn independently for each detector pixel before removal of instrumental characteristics, sky subtraction, 1D spectrum extraction and fluxing. While further data processing steps typically performed on 1D spectra, such as rebinning, usually introduce correlations to the noise across pixels, these correlations are localized to a few pixels. We assume complete independence of the noise when generating the simulated training data. \ld{I'm wondering if the noise has to be independent from pixel-to-pixel or the blindspot can learn correlated noise too.}

Classical denoiser algorithms, such as simple kernel convolution, PCA, or more sophisticated techniques such as Savitzky--Golay smoothing usually degrade the shape of absorption line profiles which explains why atmospheric parameter inference is more uncertain from smoothed spectra than from the original noisy ones. On the other hand, our network is trained with an objective to reduce the reconstruction error while the blindspot architecture is expected to ensure that the denoising does not erase or smooth out the absorption lines that later analyses depend on. We show, however, that minimizing reconstruction error alone introduces bias into the line profiles which limits the usefulness of denoised spectra for stellar parameter inference.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

In spectroscopic terms, self-supervised denoising substitues the unavailable, high-$\SNR$ ideal spectrum with information from repeated noisy measurements or neighboring pixels, and from the learned or assumed statistics of the noise. \citet{lehtinen2018noise2noise} showed, for two-dimensional images, that pairs of independent noisy views of the same underlying signal suffice to recover the clean signal under zero-mean noise, removing the need for clean references at training time but still requiring a second observation. \citet{batson2019noise2self} reduced this requirement further by formulating denoising as a $\mathcal{J}$-invariant prediction task on a single noisy image. Here $\mathcal{J}$-invariance means that the pixels are partitioned into held-out subsets and the prediction for any held-out pixel is computed only from the complementary pixels -- the noisy target pixel is therefore unavailable to the predictor. \citet{Krull2019_blindspot} introduced a concrete single-image realization in which a randomly masked target pixel is predicted from its surrounding context so the noisy target cannot be copied through the receptive field of the predictor. \citet{Laine2019_BSN} converted this masking principle into a computationally efficient feed-forward architecture by combining neural network branches whose receptive fields are restricted to disjoint half-spaces and then offset, so that the prediction at each output position never depends on the input at the same position. \citet{Krull2020_PN2V} extended the framework with an explicit pixel noise model and posterior inference rather than a single point estimate, while \citet{Xie2020_Noise2Same} revisited the underlying $\mathcal{J}$-invariance bound and replaced random masking with a self-consistency objective that does not require strict pixel exclusion.

These methods establish the noisy-only training paradigm on two-dimensional natural images, but they leave two questions open for one-dimensional stellar spectra: whether the masked context retains enough information about the central wavelength to support useful prediction (\ld{a range question}, addressed in Section~\ref{sec:theory} and \ld{Appendix~\ref{app:controls_gamma}}), and whether the resulting predictor preserves the narrow absorption features that downstream stellar parameter analysis depends on, rather than smoothing them toward the local continuum \ld{(Section~\ref{sec:results_physical})} or introducing bias so that downstream parameter estimators fail.

A separate line of work has applied neural denoisers directly to astronomical spectra, in all cases relying on simulator-supplied clean references. \citet{Pal2024_denoising_autoencoder} train a one-dimensional denoising autoencoder on paired noisy and clean simulated stellar spectra and show that the recovered spectra can speed up finding initial values for template fitting, at the cost of requiring synthetic clean targets at training time. \citet{Scourfield2023_AEgalaxies} apply the same supervised paradigm to galaxy spectra with a noise-augmented variational autoencoder and report lower emission-line flux residuals against PCA baselines. \citet{Kang2021_bayesian_spectrum_denoise} take a different route: rather than mapping noisy to clean directly, they learn a prior over clean stellar spectra from a high-$\SNR$ reference set and combine it with a generative noise model in a Bayesian denoising procedure, which still requires the high-$\SNR$ reference set during training. \citet{Gebran2025_denoising} argue on an empirical basis that denoisers, deep-learning-based or classical, cannot improve parameters inferred from denoised spectra.

The common limitation across these approaches is that the supervisory signal is available only inside the simulator or inside a high-$\SNR$ reference catalog, which is exactly the simulator-to-deployment dependency that motivates noisy-only training in this paper. Whether self-supervised blindspot training can match these reconstructions on stellar spectra, when judged jointly by aggregate reconstruction quality and by line-preservation diagnostics, has not previously been tested. If a denoiser only improves visual smoothness or pixel-level error while destroying spectral features that discriminate stellar parameters, it is not useful for further downstream analysis.

Classical spectroscopic parameter estimation methods usually address low-$\SNR$ observations through template fitting or, direct label inference from the entire spectrum or physically motivated features. Cross-correlation against a template library is the standard method for radial velocity estimation \citep{Tonry1979_xcorr,Kurtz1998_RVSAO} whereas the more generic multivariate maximum likelihood template fitting (sometimes with certain wavelength regions masked) is used to infer the rest of the parameters. Multivariate template fitting matches the observed flux directly to interpolated templates by summing up the log-likelihood of the data and the model in each spectroscopic pixel, often allowing for template mixtures, continuum terms, fluxing corrections, turbulent velocities, and dispersions, to recover stellar kinematics or population weights \citep{Koleva2009_ULySS,Cappellari2017_pPXFUpdate}. Data-driven machine learning models are also widely used to predict atmospheric parameters, such as \textit{The Cannon}, and neural spectral models, such as \textit{The Payne}, learn the relation between stellar labels and spectral fluxes, so that labels for new spectra can be inferred using a learned spectral model rather than by matching each observation to a hand-built template grid \citep{Ness2015_Cannon,Ting2019_Payne}.

The contribution of this paper sits at the intersection of these three lines, not inside any one of them. From self-supervised denoising, it inherits the blindspot construction and the requirement that the central pixel never enters into the prediction of itself. From deep-learning-based spectrum denoising, it inherits the goal of recovering line-sensitive structures at low $\SNR$ while removing the supervision dependency that limits deployment. Moreover, with respect to classical pipelines, it is positioned as an upstream pre-processing step, rather than as a complete parameters inference step.

Two questions follow that the rest of the paper addresses directly: whether one-dimensional medium-resolution stellar spectra satisfy the premise that the masked context of a spectral pixel carries enough information about the pixel itself so that the clean flux can be reconstructed from it, and whether the resulting reconstruction preserves enough information from the full spectrum to improve the error of a downstream stellar parameter estimators \ld{(Sections~\ref{sec:results_physical} and~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg})}.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Simulated Spectroscopic Observations}
\label{sec:simulations}

Our experiment uses simulated observations of medium resolution optical spectra derived from the BOSZ high-resolution synthetic stellar grid \citep{Bohlin2017,Meszaros2012}, based on ATLAS9 atmospheres \citep{Kurucz1979}. The synthetic spectrum is first convolved with the line-spread function and resampled to the detector pixels of a medium resolution spectrograph, similar to the Subaru Prime Focus Spectrograph (PFS) instrument, as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}. Then the flux is converted into physical units to match the desired broad-band magnitude. After applying the instrumental transmission function and the detector response function, the photon noise, dark noise and read-out noise variances are calculated in the unit of detector counts. Finally, assuming a Gaussian limit and independence of the noise between wavelength bins, a single noise variance is assigned to every spectral pixel. We note, that for the faint end of the simulated magnitude range, the sky emission can dominate the noise budget. We assumed moonless night conditions only.

In order to train a neural network on the simulated spectra, we normalize each spectrum by its integrated flux so that the continuum level is approximately $0.5$ across the spectrum. After normalization, both the flux and its uncertainty are scaled by the same constant independently of wavelength:
\begin{equation}
    x_{\lambda}=\frac{F_{\lambda}}{C},
    \qquad
    y_{\lambda}=\frac{F_{\lambda}+\epsilon_{F,\lambda}}{C},
    \qquad
    \sigma_{\lambda}^{2}
    =
    \frac{\sigma_{F,\lambda}^{2}}{C^{2}}.
    \label{eq:continuum_noise_scaling}
\end{equation}
Thus, the normalized noisy spectrum and the normalized uncertainty vector are on the same flux scale, and no theoretical or model continuum is fitted to the noisy or denoised spectra during evaluation.

We generate \ld{$211{,}000$} simulated spectra for training the neural network, which is split into training, validation, and test sets in a  $200{,}000$ / $1{,}000$ / $10{,}000$ ratio. The magnitude and stellar-parameter ranges match those of the motivating Galactic Archeology survey: faint, late-type spectra that remain informative despite being strongly noise dominated. Simulation parameters are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}. The parameters were sampled uniformly from their respective support intervals. In case of the training set, we generate new realizations of the noise for each spectrum and training epoch, whereas the \ld{validation} and test sets have frozen noise realizations.

We avoid continuum normalization of the simulated observations because finding the continuum at low $\SNR$ is often very challenging. Continuum is most often fitted as a smooth function, typically some low order spline, through the observed spectrum, but at low $\SNR$, the noise can significantly bias the fit, leading to systematic errors in the normalized spectrum. On the other hand, by significantly reducing the noise of the observed, fluxed spectrum, our method allows for more accurate recovery of the underlying flux, hence help find the true continuum more reliably.

\subsection{Noise Model}
The observation simulator outputs the wavelength grid, normalized clean flux $x_\lambda$, normalized noisy flux $y_\lambda$, and the per-pixel uncertainty vector $\sigma_\lambda$. The per-pixel variance is calculated as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{\lambda}^{2}
    =
    \sigma_{\mathrm{obj},\lambda}^{2}
    +
    \sigma_{\mathrm{sky},\lambda}^{2}
    +
    \sigma_{\mathrm{read},\lambda}^{2},
    \label{eq:noise_components}
\end{equation}
where each term is normalized with the same constant to match the dynamic range of the neural network input. The three terms encode noise from object photons, photon noise from the structured sky background, and detector read and dark noise as transferred by the telescope and the detector. \ld{maybe elaborate on the transfer function a bit more}

\subsection{Problem Formulation and Blindspot Objective}
\label{sec:problem_formulation}

Let $\m{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{N}$ be a clean spectrum and $\m{y}\in\mathbb{R}^{N}$ its noisy observation. In components, the noisy spectrum is
\begin{align}
    y_{\lambda} &= x_{\lambda} + \epsilon_{\lambda} \\[1em]
    \epsilon_{\lambda} &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\lambda}^{2}),
    \label{eq:noise_model_scalar}
\end{align}
where $\sigma_\lambda^2$ is the variance of the noise at wavelength bin $\lambda$ and $\lambda = 1,2,\dots, N$ indexes the wavelength bins.

The task is to learn the $\m{\theta}$ parameters of a denoiser $f_{\m{\theta}}$ from noisy spectra only: during training, $\m{y}$ is used but $\m{x}$ is not. A blindspot predictor estimates each wavelength bin from its surrounding context with the center bin excluded:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x}_{\lambda} 
        = f_{\m{\theta}}(\m{y}_{-\lambda},\m{\sigma}_{-\lambda})
\end{equation}
Here $\m{y}_{-\lambda}$ and $\m{\sigma}_{-\lambda}$ denote the receptive-field input after excluding $y_{\lambda}$ and $\sigma_{\lambda}$, whereas $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$ is the point estimate returned by the network, and $\m{\theta}$ denotes all trainable weights and biases of the denoiser. The clean reference $\m{x}$ is completely withheld during training and used only for evaluation.

We note, that in our simulations, $\m{\sigma}_{\mathrm{obj}}$ is a deterministic function of the clean flux $\m{x}$ which can have implications for the training of the blindspot denoiser when $\m{\sigma}$ is used as part of the input, as the neural network could infer the clean flux from it. To avoid the leak of information about the clean flux into the prediction, we never feed the full, per-pixel uncertainty vector $\m{\sigma}$ into the network, but instead summarize it into a single constant vector $\bar{\m{\sigma}}$, which is defined as
\begin{align}
    \hat{x}_{\lambda} 
        &= f_{\m{\theta}}(\m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma})
        \label{eq:blindspot_prediction} \\
    \bar{\sigma}^2
        &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{\lambda=1}^{N}\sigma_{\lambda}^{2}.
        \label{eq:noise_summary}
\end{align}
The scalar $\bar{\sigma}$ represents the overall noise level of the spectrum and $\bar{\sigma}$ has the same unit of flux as $y_{\lambda}$.
% We use the sum of the per-bin variances rather than the full vector $\m{\sigma}$ because the full vector contains wavelength-specific information from the noiseless simulated spectrum that could cause data leakage.
The full, pixel-wise variance vector is used only to draw $\epsilon_{\lambda}$ in Equation~\ref{eq:noise_model_scalar} and the network receives only the scalar noise summary $\bar{\sigma}$.

% \ld{review: In a real-survey deployment, the same input channel can carry the survey pipeline's noise estimate without changing the network architecture. A Bayesian fusion of the network prediction with the noisy observation is possible in principle, but because the variance of the per-pixel noise level is negligible in the present setting, we do not apply it and take the network mean $\hat{x}$ directly as the denoised output.}

We train the network with Huber loss in the form of
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}(\m{\theta};\m{y})
    &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{\lambda=1}^{N}
        \ell_{\delta}\!\left(y_{\lambda} - \hat{x}_{\lambda}\right) \\[1em]
    \ell_{\delta}(r) &=
    \begin{cases}
        \tfrac{1}{2}r^{2} & |r| \leq \delta,\\
        \delta\!\left(|r| - \tfrac{1}{2}\delta\right) & |r| > \delta.
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:self_supervised_loss}
\end{align}
The Huber loss behaves as squared-error regression for residuals with $|r|\leq\delta$, but grows only linearly for larger residuals. Therefore, the Huber loss keeps the usual mean regression behavior near the model while reducing the influence of outliers and rare pixels with large noise.

The optimization problem can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \m{\theta}^\ast = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\m{\theta}}\;\mathbb{E}_{y}\!\left[\mathcal{L}(\m{\theta};\m{y})\right],
    \label{eq:theta_star}
\end{equation}
where the expectation value is taken over the ensemble of noisy spectra. The denoised spectrum is obtained by evaluating Equation~\ref{eq:blindspot_prediction} at $\m{\theta}^\ast$.

Under the symmetric zero-mean per-pixel noise model of Equation~\ref{eq:noise_model_scalar}, and for a Huber breakpoint $\delta$ chosen to be large relative to the typical residuals, the population minimizer is approximately
\begin{equation}
    f^\ast(\m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma})
    \approx
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[y_{\lambda}\mid \m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma}\right]
    =
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[x_{\lambda}\mid \m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma}\right].
    \label{eq:population_target}
\end{equation}
The equality follows because the noise has zero conditional mean. Asymmetric or heavy-tailed noise, or a $\delta$ comparable to or smaller than the typical magnitude of the residuals, would shift the optimum toward a location determined by the outliers rather than the conditional mean.

As we mentioned in Sec~\ref{sec:simulations}, the simulated spectra have associated masks that indicate which wavelength bins are considered valid for analysis. The mask is constant across all spectra, and filter out wavelength bins where the flux is dominated by strong sky emission lines, resulting in significantly higher noise levels. To avoid extreme outliers, we leave these pixels out of the analysis. As a result, instead of reconstructing the flux strictly from the immediate neighborhood of each wavelength bin, we reconstruct the flux from a fixed number of unmasked pixels which are not necessarily adjacent to the target bin.

\subsection{Blindspot Network Architecture}
\label{sec:blindspot_algorithm}

A blindspot neural network can follow any feed-forward architecture 
as long as the forward pass ensures one invariant: the feature used to predict the flux in bin $\lambda$ is never computed from $y_{\lambda}$. When feature extraction from the input is done with a convolutional network, this property is often referred to as \textit{causality}. To compute some internal features that will be used downstream to predict $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$, the one-dimensional blindspot network uses two identical, but mirrored causal branches. The left branch calculates the feature vector $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{L} = U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{L})$, where $\m{z}^{L} = \m{y}_{<\lambda}$ is the input spectrum in the normal order of wavelength bins for all $\lambda_i < \lambda$, so the extracted features at $\lambda$ depend only on $\m{y}_{<\lambda}$. The right branch processes the wavelength-reversed spectrum for all $\lambda_i > \lambda$ and calculates $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{R} = U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{R})$, so the right-side features at $\lambda$ depend only on $\m{y}_{>\lambda}$. The prediction head, that calculates $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$, receives only the $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{L}$ and $\m{u}_{\lambda}^{R}$ feature vectors, therefore, no computational path carries $y_{\lambda}$ into $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$.

The input to the blindspot network is a 1-dimensional array $\m{y}\in\mathbb{R}^{N}$ or the stacked array $\{y_\lambda, \sigma_\lambda\}_{\lambda=1}^{N} \in\mathbb{R}^{2 \times N}$ representing the normalized flux and the pixel-wise variance, ordered by wavelength. In case of simulated spectra, to avoid data leakage, the pixel-wise variance vector $\{\sigma_\lambda\}_{\lambda=1}^{N}$ is substituted with the constant vector $\bar{\sigma}$, which is the mean of the pixel-wise variance vector. To select a specific $\lambda$ for which the prediction is made, the input is padded so that the receptive field of the network covers only $\m{y}_{<\lambda}$ and $\m{y}_{>\lambda}$, but not $y_{\lambda}$. The output of the network is $\hat{x}_\lambda$. The architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:network}. When two separate channels for the flux and the variance are used, two feature vectors are created by $U_{\m{\theta}}$, and both are fed into the pointwise head $g_{\m{\theta}}$ to predict $\hat{x}_{\lambda}$.

The blindspot network is evaluated in the following steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Route the noisy spectrum $\m{y}$ and $\bar{\m{\sigma}}$ in the two wavelength directions:
        \begin{equation*}
            \m{z}^{L}\leftarrow \{\m{y}, \bar{\m{\sigma}}\}
            \quad \text{and} \quad
            \m{z}^{R}\leftarrow \{\mathrm{rev}(\m{y}), \bar{\m{\sigma}}\}
        \end{equation*}
    \item Apply the same $U_{\m{\theta}}$ to each branch shifted to the wavelength of interest:
        \begin{equation*}
            \m{u}^{L}\leftarrow U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{L}),
            \quad
            \tilde{\m{u}}^{R}\leftarrow U_{\m{\theta}}(\m{z}^{R})
        \end{equation*}
    \item Restore the right-branch features to the original wavelength order:
        \begin{equation*}
            \m{u}^{R}\leftarrow\mathrm{rev}(\tilde{\m{u}}^{R}),
        \end{equation*}
        so $\m{u}^{L}_{\lambda}$ sees $\m{y}_{<\lambda}$ and $\m{u}^{R}_{\lambda}$ sees $\m{y}_{>\lambda}$ only.
    \item Concatenate the two feature vectors:
        \begin{equation*}
            \m{h} \leftarrow [\m{u}^{L},\m{u}^{R}]
        \end{equation*}
    \item Predict the denoised flux at each wavelength bin:
        \begin{equation*}
            \hat{x}_{\lambda}\leftarrow g_{\theta}(\m{h})
        \end{equation*}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Identifiability and Recoverability}
\label{sec:theory}

Throughout this section we work under the heteroscedastic Gaussian noise model of Section~\ref{sec:problem_formulation}, where the noise has zero conditional mean even though its variance depends on the wavelength and the flux. We also assume that the noise in adjacent pixels is uncorrelated.

\paragraph{Blindspot exclusion prevents the identity shortcut}
If the center pixel was visible, the noisy-target objective would have a zero-loss copy solution:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x}_{\lambda}=y_{\lambda}
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    \mathcal{L}(\m{\theta};\m{y})
    = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\lambda=1}^{L}
        \ell_{\delta}\!\left(y_{\lambda}-y_{\lambda}\right)
    =0.
\end{equation}
This solution contains no information about $x_{\lambda}$. The blindspot construction eliminates this problem by explicitly excluding $y_{\lambda}$ from the receptive field of the network.

\paragraph{Identifiability follows from zero-mean noise} Let $\Omega_{\lambda} = (\m{y}_{-\lambda},\bar{\sigma})$ denote the blindspot context. Identifiability follows from
\begin{align}
    y_{\lambda} = x_{\lambda} + \epsilon_{\lambda} \\[1em]
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[\epsilon_{\lambda} \mid x_{\lambda},\Omega_{\lambda}\right] = 0,
    \label{eq:consistency_setup}
\end{align}
which gives
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[y_{\lambda}\mid \Omega_{\lambda}\right]
    \;=\;
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[x_{\lambda}\mid \Omega_{\lambda}\right].
    \label{eq:consistency}
\end{equation}
The unpredictable noise washes out under the conditional expectation, leaving the context-conditioned clean signal as the optimal regression target.

\paragraph{Loss function caveat}
Equation~\ref{eq:consistency} identifies the conditional mean as the population minimizer under squared loss. Our training objective uses the Huber loss $\ell_{\delta}$ in Equation~\ref{eq:self_supervised_loss} for robustness against outliers. As $\delta\to\infty$, this loss reduces to the MSE and the population optimum coincides with the conditional mean, while at finite $\delta$, the optimum is a Huber M-estimator that approximates the conditional mean when the the distribution of the residuals is symmetric and light-tailed relative to the breakpoint. Therefore, we treat Equation~\ref{eq:consistency} as the leading-order target, rather than an exact identity.

\paragraph{Recoverability is measured by the context-residual ratio}
The blindspot construction is useful only when the spectral pixels surrounding the blind spot determine the clean flux at the hidden wavelength better than the noisy observation itself. We measure the recoverability by the ratio between two variances: let $\tau_\lambda^{2}$ denote the conditional variance of the clean flux after observing the blindspot context, whereas we keep using $\sigma_\lambda^{2}$ to denote the variance of the noise in the withheld pixel:
\begin{align}
    \tau_\lambda^{2} &= \mathrm{Var}[x_{\lambda}\mid\Omega_{\lambda}] \\[1em]
    \sigma_\lambda^{2} &= \mathrm{Var}[\epsilon_{\lambda}].
\end{align}
With these, we define the context-residual ratio in the form of
\begin{align}
    \gamma_\lambda &=\; \frac{\tau_\lambda^{2}}{\sigma_\lambda^{2}}.
    \label{eq:gamma}
\end{align}
A small $\gamma_\lambda$ means the context predicts the clean flux more accurately than the noisy center observation itself, so the real flux at the hidden wavelength is recoverable in the sense needed for blindspot denoising.

\subsection{The size of the target support}

The target support, i.e. the set of neighboring pixels that carry information about the flux in a withheld pixel, has a physically determined size. The reason to expect a small $\gamma_\lambda$ for a medium resolution spectrograph in case of only 15-25 neighboring pixels is not the generic smoothness of stellar spectra, but the ratio between the spectral correlation length and the variance of the noise. The spectral correlation length is determined jointly by stellar physics via intrinsic line widths and by the instrument via the line spread function (LSF).

\subsection{Information limits on downstream tasks}

In order to estimate a stellar parameter $\varphi$ from a noisy spectrum, we consider the Fisher information matrix $\mathcal{I}_{\varphi}$, which quantifies the amount of information that a single noisy observation $\m{y}$ carries about the parameter $\phi$. For scalar $\varphi$, the Fisher information takes the form of
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}_{\varphi}(\m{y}) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \varphi}\log p(\m{y}\mid\varphi)\right)^{2}\right],
\end{equation}
where $p(\m{y}\mid\varphi)$ is the likelihood of the noisy observation $\m{y}$ given the parameter $\varphi$ and the expectation is taken over all noise realizations. The Fisher information matrix is a lower bound on the variance of any unbiased estimator of $\varphi$ through the Cramér--Rao bound. The denoiser network $f_\theta$ is a deterministic transformation of the noisy observation $\m{y}$ into a denoised spectrum $\hat{\m{x}}$, hence the information about $\varphi$ in the denoised spectrum $\hat{\m{x}}$ cannot exceed the information in the noisy observation $\m{y}$. This is expressed by the data processing inequality
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}_{\varphi}(\m{y}) \geq \mathcal{I}_{\varphi}(\hat{\m{x}}),
\end{equation}
which inequality is strict unless $f_\theta$ is invertible, but this is not the case for a blindspot denoiser. Therefore a denoised spectrum cannot contain more information about a parameter than the particular noisy spectrum from which it was computed.

What the denoiser can exploit is not extra information in that one spectrum, but the ensemble information contained in the training set. Across training examples, the network sees many independent noise realizations drawn on spectra from the same physical support, and therefore learns a population prior for what spectra typically look like. At inference time, this prior can shrink a noisy realization toward the high-probability manifold of the training ensemble, reducing estimator variance at the cost of bias. Any apparent downstream gain must therefore be interpreted as a bias--variance tradeoff induced by the training distribution, not as creation of new information in a single observation.

In the present benchmark, however, the training, validation, and test splits are drawn from the same physical parameter range. The denoiser therefore does not inject label information from a mismatched external population; it uses the structure of the traning set ensemble of simulations to regularize each noisy spectrum. This can make downstream estimators more stable, but it does not evade the information bound above. The Fisher information matrix therefore remains the theoretical upper bound on the performance of any downstream estimator that uses the denoised spectrum $\hat{\m{x}}$ to estimate the parameter $\varphi$.

\paragraph{From pixel recoverability to downstream parameter estimation}
The recoverability ratio in Equation~\ref{eq:gamma} concerns pixel-level reconstruction. Downstream tasks, such as atmospheric parameter estimation or equivalent width measurement, see Sections~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg}~and~\ref{sec:metrics}, are non-linear functionals of the full spectrum or parts of the spectrum, and are not directly bounded by $\gamma$. The EW-variance reduction factor is approximately a weighted average of the $\gamma_\lambda$ over the window where the EW is proportional to the reduced error in the form of
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Var}[\mathrm{EW}] \propto \sum_{\lambda\in W} \gamma_\lambda \sigma_\lambda^2.
\end{equation}
If the denoiser introduces coherent bias, for example by slightly filling in line wings, then $\mathbb{E}[\Delta \mathrm{EW}]$, the expected bias in the equivalent width, will be proportional to the sum of the per-pixel biases:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\Delta \mathrm{EW}] \propto
    \sum_{\lambda\in W}\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_{\lambda}],
\end{equation}
and this bias can remain substantial even when all $\gamma_\lambda$ are small. So $\gamma$ carries over to to EW as a predictor of the variance reduction, but not necessarily of the bias. We note that this approximation is simplified as we did not take the error of continuum estimation and normalization into account but if we did it would increase both the variance and the bias of the equivalent width measurements.

Hence, instead of claiming that the denoiser preserves, let alone enhances the information about the stellar parameters, we only make the weaker claim that a blindspot denoiser can recover the line shapes well enough so that a downstream non-linear regressor, trained solely on clean spectra and known labels, can make use of a denoised spectrum without retraining.

As we will show in Section~\ld{XX}, while the error of equivalent width measurements from a denoised spectrum, with respect to the clean reference, is significantly smaller than that from noisy spectra, the results are biased. Line shape appears to be preserved to leading order, but the denoiser does not recover the full information content of the lines. For example, strong bias is clearly visible in the wings of Ca\,\textsc{ii} lines which are, in case of metal-poor giant stars, sensitive to surface gravity. This foreshadows that the denoiser will not recover the information about $\log g$ either.

\subsection{Training}
\label{sec:training}
The denoiser network we train consists of a single U-Net backbone $U_{\m{\theta}}$ with four input channels for feature extraction and and a prediction head of convolutional layers. The backbone has 9 convolutional layers with a kernel size of $25$ and a total number of 48 kernels. The input channels to the U-Net are the left and right shifted causal branches with proper padding and the two channels of constant vectors filled with the scalar noise parameter $\bar{\sigma}$. The U-Net backbone has an output of 2 times 48 channels, the $\m{u}^{L}$ and $\m{u}^{R}$ feature vectors, which are concatenated into a single channel by the prediction head. 
%In the U-Net backbone, we use batch normalization and \ld{residual aggregation between matching encoder and decoder levels}.
In the U-Net backbone, each decoder level upsamples the coarse feature map, concatenates it with the corresponding encoder feature map through a standard skip connection, and applies two batch-normalized 1D convolutional blocks.
The prediction head $g_{\m{\theta}}$ consists of 3 convolutional layers with a kernel size of $1$ to mix down the U-Net output to a single channel that predicts the denoised flux $\hat{x}$. The U-Net backbone uses ReLU activation.

The networks is trained with the Huber loss of Eq.~\ref{eq:self_supervised_loss} with \ld{$\delta=0.1$}. Optimization is done using an Adam optimizer with $(\beta_1,\beta_2,\epsilon)=(0.9,0.999,10^{-8})$ and zero weight decay, initial learning rate $10^{-4}$, and a cosine schedule decaying to $10^{-6}$ over the full training horizon. Training uses a maximum of $200$ epochs with a batch size of $128$ and a single fixed training seed. The early-stopping patience is set to $30$ epochs and the model with the smallest Huber loss on the validation set is chosen as the best-fit model, before any test set evaluation. At each training step, $85\%$ of wavelength bins are sampled at random and contribute to the loss function, while the remaining bins are held out. This stochastic masking is standard for training blindspot networks in order to decorrelate the predictions for successive wavelength bins. During test set evaluation, the full spectra are used to calculate the Huber loss. We note, that while the Huber loss is used for training the network, we report the RMSE of the denoised spectra in the results section, which is a more standard metric for the reconstruction error and does not scale down the effect of outliers.

The detailed training procedure is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item For each epoch and each mini-batch of noisy spectra, calculate the scalar noise parameter
        \begin{equation*}
            \left( \bar{\sigma}^{(j)} \right)^2 = L^{-1}\sum_{\lambda} \left( \sigma_{\lambda}^{(j)} \right)^{2}.
        \end{equation*}
        A constant vector $\bar{\m{\sigma}}^{(j)}$ formed from this value is used as the second input channel to the network.
    \item Calculate the prediction for each wavelength bin with a blindspot forward pass:
        \begin{equation*}
            \hat{x}_{\lambda}^{(j)} = f_{\theta}(\m{y}_{-\lambda}^{(j)},\bar{\m{\sigma}}^{(j)}).
        \end{equation*}
    \item Generate a random mask of $85\%$ of the wavelength bins and calculate the Huber loss of Equation~\ref{eq:self_supervised_loss} only for the masked bins.
    \item For each mini-batch, calculate the gradient and update the network parameters $\theta$ with the Adam optimizer.
    \item After each epoch, evaluate the Huber loss on the validation set and save the model if it is the best so far.
    \item Repeat until early stopping criteria are met or a maximum number of epochs is reached.
\end{enumerate}

We train the blindspot denoiser in two configurations: one with a single-channel input for the noisy flux only, and one with a two-channel input for the noisy flux and the constant $\bar{\sigma}$ vector which is just a summary of the per-pixel variance vector. While the difference in performance is not significant, including the noise parameter as an additional input slightly help with reconstruction, see Sec.~\ld{XX}.

\subsection{Baseline Models for Comparison}
\label{sec:baseline_models}

To establish a baseline for the blindspot denoiser, we compare it to three reference methods.

The first baseline model is a classic PCA-based denoiser that uses a clean reference library to learn a low-dimensional linear subspace of the simulated spectra in which the majority of the variance of the clean spectra is captured. The noisy input spectra are then projected onto this subspace to obtain a denoised reconstruction. To test the overall denoising capabilities of the PCA-based method, we set the number of principal components to just $k=16$, which captures more than $99\%$ of the variance in the clean spectra but is known to be insufficient to capture the full complexity of stellar absorption features -- \ld{CITE}.

The second benchmark is a Savitzky--Golay smoothing filter applied directly to the noisy spectra. Savitzky--Golay smoothing is a classical signal-processing technique that fits a low-degree polynomial to the values of the input signal in a moving window, and replaces the center point with the value of the fitted polynomial. For our particular application, we used a Savitzky--Golay filter with a window size of \ld{$11$} and a polynomial order of \ld{$3$}. \ld{-- verify the numbers}

The third benchmark is a supervised U-Net denoising autoencoder that is trained on paired noisy-clean spectra, similar to the method of \citet{Pal2024_denoising_autoencoder}, except using a different network architecture where the output does not have the blindspot constraint on the receptive field and can use the full noisy spectrum as input, including the observed value of each pixel, to predict the noiseless pixel values.

\subsection{Metrics for Performance Evaluation}
\label{sec:metrics}

We use three metrics to evaluate the performance of the denoiser: the reconstruction RMSE, the relative equivalent width error of the Ca\,\textsc{ii} triplet lines, and the relative equivalent width error of the \mbox{O$_2$} A-band absorption, as well as the performance of a $\log g$ regressor applied to the denoised spectra. The first metric is a standard measure of reconstruction quality, while the latter are physically motivated metrics that measure how well the denoiser preserves astrophysically relevant features in the spectra.

\paragraph{Reconstruction error}
Since, in case of simulated spectra, we know the clean reference, as well as the noise variance as a function of wavelength, we use the usual definition of the RMSE to compare the quality of the denoised spectrum to the original and the clean reference spectrum:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RMSE}(z)
    =
    \frac{\lVert \m{z} - \m{x} \rVert_{2}}{\sqrt{N}},
    \label{eq:rmse_def}
\end{equation}
where $z$ can be the noisy input $y$ or the denoised output $\hat{x}$, and $N$ is the number of wavelength bins in the spectrum.

We use the usual definition of equivalent width with locally-fitted linear continua around each absorption line in the form of

\end{equation}

The denominator $c_\lambda$ is the linear continuum.

\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{\mathrm{EW}}(z;\lambda_{1},\lambda_{2})

\end{equation}

\subsection{Downstream $\log g$ Estimation}

Surface gravity measurement is a key step in target selection for Galactic Archeology when targeting distant giant stars is the goal and foreground dwarf contamination must be removed. Surface gravity measurement is difficult from low-$\SNR$ spectra, so a denoiser that preserves the line shapes could be useful to detect mis-targeted stars early on into the observations from a few short, noisy exposures. Identifying wrong targets early, and repositioning the fibers could help avoid wasting valuable telescope time on less interesting targets.

On the other hand, a pixel-level reconstruction gain is useful for Galactic Archaeology only if the denoised spectrum conveys enough information to a downstream estimator to make more accurate predictions from the denoised spectrum than from the noisy one. We, therefore, test the denoiser in a $\log g$ estimation task, where the goal is to predict the surface gravity of a star from its spectrum.

% LightGBM paper:
% https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/lightgbm.pdf?msockid=1d869ab6dfab6add27cf8dcdde126b49

We project the spectra to a $k=200$ PCA basis to extract features and apply the LightGBM \citep{2017Ke_LightGBM} model, a classical decision tree regressor that uses gradient boosting, to learn non-linear relationships between the input features and the target variable. The LightGBM model is trained to predict $\log g$ from the entire wavelength range of the simulated spectra. To avoid overfitting, the $\log g$ training is done on almost clean spectra, with the noise multiplied by $10^{-3}$ compared to the realistic simulations. The performance of LightGBM is evaluated on the same test spectra as the blindspot denoiser; with different noise levels, without re-training, as it is plotted in Fig.~\ref{fig:lightgbm}. While the model performs fairly well on almost clean spectra, its performance degrades quickly as the level of noise increases, highlighting the challenge of $\log g$ estimation from noisy spectra.

To measure the goodness of parameter prediction, we use the coefficient of determination $R^2$, which measures the proportion of variance in the true $\log g$ values that is explained by the predicted values. It is defined as
\begin{equation}
    R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \hat{x}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \bar{x})^2},
\end{equation}
where $x_i$ is the true value, $\hat{x}_i$ is the predicted value, $\bar{x}$ is the mean of the true values, and $N$ is the number of data points. An $R^2$ value of $1$ indicates perfect predictions, while a value of $0$ indicates that the model does no better than predicting the mean of the true values.

\ld{Let's make a twist here. It's clear that any estimator that works on the full spectrum will be biased on the full spectrum because the denoiser cannot put information into the spectrum. But if we have a parameter estimator that works on small parts of the spectrum (EW, chemical abundances, etc), it actually could improve the estimator's performance.}

\ld{Since a denoiser cannot create new information that is not present in the noisy spectrum, it still could concentrate the information into features that help infer physical parameters and perform classification jobs. We argue, that either parallel traning of a denoiser and a regressor/classifier, on noisy spectra and known labels, or training a regressor to predict from the latent variables of the blindspot network could improve physical parameter inference.}

In this simulated target support, noisy-only blindspot training lowers the mean reconstruction RMSE from $0.084$ on the noisy input to $0.0053$ on test spectra, below validation-tuned classical filters, noisy-PCA, and a non-deployable clean-template PCA reference. The line-sensitive diagnostics in Section~\ref{sec:results_physical} show that this gain is not obtained by simply filling absorption features toward the continuum: Ca\,\textsc{ii} EW errors decrease substantially. The full-spectrum calibrator in Section~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg} then provides the end-to-end result: after training only on clean spectra, the $\log g$ estimator reads substantially more label information from blindspot-denoised spectra than from noisy or conventionally smoothed spectra.

\paragraph{Rest-frame alignment as a deployment cost.} The reconstruction ceiling of the linear baselines in Table~\ref{tab:main_baselines} --- a property of the estimator class rather than of tuning, as Appendix~\ref{app:controls_gamma} shows --- is sometimes read as a mere preprocessing gap: shift every spectrum to its rest frame first, and the basis would no longer have to span a separate velocity copy of each line. That repair is not available in the setting this paper targets. Rest-frame resampling needs the per-spectrum radial velocity, and recovering it without a clean template reintroduces the supervised dependency that the noisy-only construction is built to remove. At the faint magnitudes that motivate denoising, a velocity estimated from the noisy spectrum itself is uncertain, and that uncertainty propagates into every realigned pixel. Granting an exact velocity does not make the alignment free either. Resampling onto a common grid is an interpolation that forms each output pixel from a weighted sum of neighbors. This couples the previously independent per-pixel noise draws that the consistency argument of Section~\ref{sec:theory} depends on, and it low-pass filters the narrow line cores, broadening and shallowing exactly the high-frequency, low-variance structure the fixed basis already cannot recover. Alignment thus either reintroduces clean-reference supervision or degrades the signal it is meant to expose. The blindspot estimator never faces this choice: it predicts each bin from the local shape of its neighborhood with the center withheld, so a global velocity shift slides the evaluation window without altering the learned line-shape-to-flux map. Its invariance to radial velocity is built into the estimator rather than supplied by a preprocessing stage that must first be made to work.

\paragraph{Scope of the simulated benchmark.} The evaluated setting is the independent zero-mean simulator of Section~\ref{sec:method}, with continuum-normalized late-type spectra and the PFS-like wavelength, resolution, and magnitude range of Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}. Real survey deployment introduces correlated sky-subtraction residuals, extraction covariance, and a varying instrumental line-spread function that are not part of the noise model tested here. Continuum-estimation error is also out of scope: the analysis is conditioned on continuum-normalized spectra produced by the simulator, so the systematic continuum-fit error that real surveys must absorb does not enter any of the metrics. Carrying the same construction to survey-specific residuals or to other stellar supports requires reusing the same evaluation protocol: select the denoiser without clean targets, hold it fixed, and then test reconstruction, line preservation, and downstream clean-trained transfer on a disjoint evaluation set.

\paragraph{Reproducibility scope.} All main numerical results are obtained from one selected checkpoint evaluated on the z1/test\_10k\_1 split, with a single noise realization per spectrum. The repeated-noise diagnostic in Appendix~\ref{app:noise_stability} tests sensitivity to noise draws for a fixed clean spectrum; it is not uncertainty calibration and it does not substitute for a full training-seed robustness study. Independent training-seed repeats, scalar-noise-conditioning ablations, and supervised clean-target references are left outside the main claim unless evaluated under the same pre-specified metric battery.

\paragraph{Copy-shortcut control.} The copy shortcut is ruled out structurally by the blindspot construction and numerically by the integrity audit detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:architecture_integrity}; we do not run a separate central-visible/noisy-target reconstruction control.

\paragraph{Downstream information preservation.} The clean-trained $\log g$ calibrator reads from the full flux vector rather than from the six equivalent-width diagnostics, and is never retrained on noisy or denoised spectra. Its lower residuals on blindspot-denoised input therefore record a transfer property of the denoised representation, not a tuning artefact. The validation-tuned smoothers, scored against the same fixed calibrator, do not produce the same improvement, which separates the line-preserving content of the blindspot reconstruction from generic smoothness. A reduction in $\log g$ residual scatter is not the same object as a thresholded dwarf--giant confusion matrix; that classification metric is not reported here.

\paragraph{Estimator-dependent score.} The downstream gain reported here is measured under a full-spectrum gradient-boosted regressor trained on clean spectra and evaluated without refitting. Line-window-restricted tangent-risk diagnostics in companion benchmark work can report different scores under the same simulator family because they index the denoiser error by a declared local-linear evaluation map rather than by a full-spectrum nonlinear parameter calibrator. The two readings should not be averaged or substituted for one another: the present result supports usefulness for this $\log g$ calibrator, while line-window diagnostics answer a different local-fidelity question.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This study shows that a one-dimensional blindspot denoiser can turn noisy late-type spectra into reconstructions that preserve absorption-line structure and improve clean-trained gravity transfer on a controlled BOSZ/PFS-like benchmark. Clean spectra do not enter the denoiser training loss, and the target wavelength is excluded from the receptive field. Under the selected checkpoint and fixed evaluation protocol, the evidence separates into three claims: reconstruction improves, line diagnostics remain favorable, and a surface-gravity estimator trained on clean spectra transfers better without refitting.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reconstruction gain.} On the $10{,}000$-spectrum reconstruction test split, mean per-spectrum reconstruction RMSE falls from $0.084$ for the noisy input to $0.0053$ for the blindspot output, a factor of about $16$. The central-visible control remains at the noisy-input scale ($0.084$), supporting the interpretation that target-bin exclusion prevents a copy shortcut rather than merely adding network capacity.
    \item \textbf{Line preservation.} Median Ca\,\textsc{ii} relative EW errors for the $8498$, $8542$, and $8662$~\AA{} lines fall to $0.121$, $0.098$, and $0.147$. These diagnostics argue that the reconstruction retains local absorption-line contrast rather than only improving the global norm.
    \item \textbf{Estimator transfer.} For a full-spectrum surface-gravity estimator trained on clean spectra and evaluated without refitting on a disjoint $5{,}000$-spectrum test partition, robust scatter falls from $1.26$ to $0.80$~dex and $R^{2}$ rises from $0.01$ to $0.46$. This is an estimator-specific transfer gain, not full stellar-label recovery.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these results make the case for blindspot denoising as a candidate preprocessing step for faint late-type spectra, with usefulness measured by fixed downstream transfer rather than by visual smoothness alone. The natural next validation is to repeat the same fixed-estimator transfer test on observed spectra, or on simulations that include structured sky residuals, extraction covariance, line-spread-function variation, and continuum-estimation error.

\paragraph{Consolidated per-method results.}
Reconstruction RMSE decreases with estimator capacity, but the downstream $\log g$ estimator separates methods that preserve label information from methods that only reduce pixel error: the principal-component references reach low reconstruction RMSE among the non-neural methods yet reduce the fixed calibrator's downstream accuracy relative to the noisy-input row, whereas the blindspot moves the calibrator most of the way to the clean-input scale.

\paragraph{Why the fixed-basis linear estimators saturate.} The reconstruction ceiling of the principal-component baselines in Table~\ref{tab:main_baselines} is a property of the estimator class, not of how the basis was tuned. A fixed-grid linear basis must spend rank on two demands at once. Each spectrum carries a radial velocity of up to $\pm300$~km/s (Table~\ref{tab:data_summary}), which slides its absorption lines by several resolution elements, and representing an arbitrary shift on a fixed wavelength grid requires many Fourier modes rather than a few stellar-parameter directions. The narrow line cores are themselves high-frequency, low-variance features that again demand high rank. Both pressures push toward a large basis. Yet at the faint-end per-pixel $\SNR$ of this sample, every added component admits more noise variance than line variance, so the reconstruction-error-optimal rank is small: validation selects $k=16$ across the search range $\{16,\dots,512\}$. The linear class is therefore caught in a bias--variance bind, where the rank the signal structure demands is the rank the noise forbids, and no choice of rank --- with or without access to clean training targets --- escapes it. This is why the clean-template reference, fit on noiseless spectra, still floors at a reconstruction RMSE of $0.011$. The blindspot estimator is not a fixed-basis projection: it predicts each bin from the local shape of its neighborhood with the center withheld, so its accuracy is set by the physical correlation length of the signal relative to the per-bin noise (Section~\ref{sec:theory}) rather than by a subspace dimension, and a global velocity shift leaves the learned line-shape-to-flux map unchanged.

\paragraph{Heteroscedasticity and scalar $\gamma$.}
\label{app:gamma_heteroscedasticity}
The simulator draws $\epsilon_{\lambda}$ with the per-bin variance $\sigma_{\lambda}^{2}$ of Equation~\ref{eq:noise_components}, while the denoiser receives only the spectrum-level RMS noise condition $\bar{\sigma}$ of Equation~\ref{eq:blindspot_prediction}. The reported $\gamma$ values are therefore pixelwise residual-variance ratios summarized over wavelength, not a homoscedastic noise assumption. Windows whose variance profile differs from the spectrum-level summary are checked explicitly through the Ca\,\textsc{ii}, \mbox{O$_2$}, and TiO columns of Table~\ref{tab:gamma_full} and through the line-preservation diagnostics in Section~\ref{sec:results_physical}.

\paragraph{Risk interpretation of $\gamma$.}
\label{app:gamma_risk_interpretation}
The main text uses $\gamma$ only as a recoverability condition. In the local Gaussian model, the same ratio also gives the explicit comparison among three estimators of $x_{\lambda}$:
\[
    R_{\rm raw} = \sigma^{2},
    \qquad
    R_{\rm BS} = \tau^{2} = \gamma\,\sigma^{2},
    \qquad
    R_{\rm full} = \frac{\gamma}{1+\gamma}\,\sigma^{2}.
\]
The corresponding ratios are
\[
    \frac{R_{\rm raw}}{R_{\rm BS}} = \frac{1}{\gamma},
    \qquad
    \frac{R_{\rm BS}}{R_{\rm full}} = 1+\gamma,
    \qquad
    \frac{R_{\rm raw}}{R_{\rm full}} = 1 + \frac{1}{\gamma}.
\]
Thus, when $\gamma\ll1$, the context-only blindspot conditional-mean estimator is close to the center-visible conditional-mean estimator, while both are far better than leaving the noisy pixel unchanged. This calculation is only an interpretation of the measured context-residual ratio; it is not used as a substitute for the test-set reconstruction and line-preservation tests in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_ew_panel} resolves the line-preservation result of Section~\ref{sec:results_physical} into the dispersion of the EW relative error across diagnostic features as input $\SNR$ varies. \section{Fixed-Spectrum Repeated-Noise Stability}
\label{app:noise_stability}

This appendix records the repeated-noise diagnostic referenced from Section~\ref{sec:results_downstream_logg} and from the reproducibility scope paragraph of Section~\ref{sec:discussion}. The main-text reconstruction and downstream calibration use one selected trained model; we test whether the reported point estimates are stable to repeated noise perturbations of a fixed clean spectrum. Holding one test clean spectrum $x$ fixed (the representative spectrum of Figure~\ref{fig:representative_spectrum}), we draw $10^{4}$ independent noise realizations from the same heteroscedastic simulator, denoise each realization, and summarize the output ensemble by its mean $\langle\hat{x}\rangle$ and across-draw spread $\sigma_{\hat{x}}$.

% ROLE: fixed-spectrum noise-draw stability only; not uncertainty calibration or training-seed robustness.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/fig2_noise_ensemble.pdf}
\caption{Fixed-spectrum repeated-noise diagnostic for the reported blindspot model. The representative test spectrum of Figure~\ref{fig:representative_spectrum} is perturbed by $10^{4}$ independent noise draws and denoised repeatedly. \textbf{Top-left:} clean spectrum $x$ and repeated-draw mean estimate $\langle \hat{x} \rangle$, shifted for readability. \textbf{Bottom-left:} residual $x-\langle \hat{x} \rangle$. \textbf{Right:} input noise scale and across-draw spread $\sigma_{\hat{x}}$ of the denoised outputs on a shared linear axis. This diagnostic measures sensitivity to noise realizations for a fixed spectrum; it is not a calibrated uncertainty estimate.}
\label{fig:noise_ensemble}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:noise_ensemble} shows that the repeated-draw mean follows the clean spectrum and that the denoised spread remains far below the input noise scale over most wavelengths for this fixed spectrum. Taking the across-draw spread $\sigma_{\hat{x}}$ as the output noise and the clean flux as the numerator, the median over the unmasked pixels, $\mathrm{median}_{\lambda}(x_{\lambda}/\sigma_{\hat{x},\lambda})$, gives an output $\SNR$ of about $130$ for this spectrum ($116$ when restricted to the Ca\,\textsc{ii} triplet window), against $3.2$ at the input under the same per-pixel definition; these are the values quoted in Figure~\ref{fig:representative_spectrum}. The diagnostic supports stability to repeated simulator noise draws in this example only; it should not be read as posterior uncertainty calibration, an error bar on individual line measurements, population robustness, or robustness to all training seeds.
